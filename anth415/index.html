The Fascinating Discovery of Homo Naledi

Discovery

In October 2013, explorers first unearthed specimens of the now-extinct hominid species Homo Naledi from the Rising Star Cave System in South Africa. Since then, this discovery has yielded the largest collection of hominind fossils ever found in Africa representing a single species. [2]

Two field expeditions occurred to explore the site where Homo Naledi was found, one in November 2013 and the other in March 2014. During these trips, the team uncovered a small supply of faunal bones, along with a great number of human remains. Researchers place the total number of hominin specimens uncovered at 1550, representing at least 15 different individuals of the Homo Naledi species. [1]

Location

The Rising Star Cave System is located in Cradle of Humankind, South Africa. The site is split into two chambers: the Dinaledi chamber and the Lesedi chamber. Both are located approximately 30 meters below ground. [1]

The Lesedi chamber is named for the word "light" in Setswana, a local language. [3] Meanwhile, Dinaledi roughly translates to "Chamber of Stars" in the Sotho language. [2] Homo Naledi follows this linguistic pattern. The word "naledi" means "star" in Sotho, which is a reference to the Dinaledi chamber where the first specimens were discovered. [1]

Skull Features

The skull features of Homo Naledi are one of the most fascinating takeaways from this discovery. When skulls of this hominin were uncovered, they were suprisingly well-preserved by the conditions of the cave. Researchers were able to re-fit nearly all of the skull components. [3]

The discovery of Homo Naledi is notable for the extrodinary quantity and quality of bone samples recovered from the cave system. Show left are some of these specimens. Researchers have drawn many comparisons between Homo Naledi bones and those of other hominin species and anatomically modern humans. For example, the curved fingers, hip joints, small brain size, and other forms closely resemble early species such as Homo Habilis. With Neanderthals, Homo Naledi bears similarity at the wrist, hands, legs, and feet. [4] Some researchers have suggested that these limbs might even be compared to modern humans in terms of manipulatory adaptations. [1]

Dating

Shown right is a schematic of the Lesedi chamber, highlighting the locations where specimens were uncovered. As of 2017, material from this chamber remains undated. This is primarly attributed to the complex stratigraphy and sediment erosion. This has caused the obstruction of common dating methods and ambugitity around the original depositional context of the specimens. [3]

The best estimates for the age of Homo Naledi are between 100,000 to 2 million years ago. Some researchers recommend alternative methods of dating, such as radiocarbon dating or DNA sample analysis. Unfortunately, such alternative attempts have been lacking. [4] While Homo Naledi is certainly an amazing discovery, this shortcoming leaves a lot to be desired.

Mysteries

Aside from the size and recency of Homo Naledi's discovery, the curious circumstances to how the specimens might have arrived in the cave system is another subject of interest. Researches have completely ruled out a few theories, such as the homininds living in the cave or being killed, then left there, by predators. [4] The larger number of fossils in close proximity, lack of animal remains, and the lack of specimen damage from animals strongly suggests the latter theory is false. [2]

One theory that was originally considered is the possibility of a flood, which would have washed the hominins into the cave system. However, this theory is not consistent with the intactness of the bodies in the chamber. There is also no evidence that the specimens were at any point exposed to the surface. [3]

The most popular present theory is that the arrival of the hominin specimens to the cave system was an example of early burial practices, which is considered suprisingly complex behavior given their small brain size. [4] In fact, no other hominins in the region have been found to have engaged in such practices, which perhaps indicates a perviously unknown and advanced level of cultural development. The quality of the specimens also suggests that the bodies were deposited not by dropping them down an entrance, but by deliberate and careful placement. While there are some questionable variables, such as the lack of a vertical passageway into the Dinaledi Chamber, this theory is considered the most plausible and suggests that the species was more complex than what previously might have been projected. [2]

While this theory, if true, could be an extrodinary discovery for the anthropological record, it is not without its criticism. This primarily includes the practicality of burial practices given the conditions of the cave. For example, access to the chambers are so narrow they barely permit a single person to enter. Moving the bodies would require great effort, and it must have been done in a way that would not damage them. [5] There may never be a clear answer to explain how Homo Naledi arrived in the cave system, but the optimisitc view of most researchers could point to groundbreaking developments in the biocultural evolution of pre-human species.

Significance

The expeditions that uncovered Homo Naledi have, in many ways, brought great findings to the field of anthropology. Both the quantity and preservation of the specimens have allowed researchers to draw many conclusions about the history and development of hominin species. The lack of precise dating methods, however, has continued to leave some facts uncertain. Nevertheless, the remaining mysteries surrounding observation of the cave system, and the possibility of complex behavior such as burials taking place, makes Homo Naledi a truly fascinating discovery
